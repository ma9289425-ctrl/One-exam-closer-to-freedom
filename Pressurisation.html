1 — Atmospheric Pressure & Altitude

Standard pressure values and the effect of altitude on flight

▼
💡 Key Concept

Air has weight. As altitude increases, there is less air above — so atmospheric pressure decreases.

Standard Sea-Level Pressure

UnitValue
PSI14.7 psi
inHg29.92 inHg
mmHg760 mmHg
hPa / mb1013.2 hPa

Temperature & Altitude

Troposphere

Where most civil aviation occurs.

Temperature decreases with altitude at −2°C per 1,000 ft (standard lapse rate).

Tropopause

Avg: ~38,000 ft

Poles: 25,000–30,000 ft

Equator: up to 60,000 ft

Temp at tropopause: ~−57°C

Why Aircraft Fly High

  • Lower air density → reduced drag
  • Improved fuel efficiency
  • Avoidance of weather and turbulence
  • Pressurisation makes the environment safe and comfortable
⚡ Quick Recap

Standard sea-level pressure = 14.7 psi / 29.92 inHg / 1013.2 hPa. Lapse rate = −2°C per 1,000 ft. Tropopause ≈ 38,000 ft at approx. −57°C.

2 — Key Pressurisation Terms

Definitions you must know for the exam

▼

Cabin Altitude

The equivalent altitude matching the pressure inside the cabin.

Example: 10.92 psi inside ≈ 8,000 ft cabin altitude.

Differential Pressure

Cabin Pressure − Ambient Pressure

Measured in psid.

Indicates the structural load on the fuselage.

Cabin Rate of Climb

Rate of cabin altitude change.

Measured in fpm (feet per minute).

Typical: 300–500 fpm.

⚡ Quick Recap

Cabin Altitude = pressure inside expressed as altitude. Diff. Pressure = cabin minus ambient (psid). Cabin Rate = change in cabin altitude (fpm).

3 — Purpose of a Pressurisation System

What the system must achieve

▼
  • Maintain cabin altitude at ~8,000 ft or lower
  • Prevent rapid or uncomfortable pressure changes
  • Provide sufficient oxygen pressure for passengers and crew
  • Remove stale air and odours
  • Support cabin heating and cooling
💡 Key Concept

The fuselage acts as a sealed pressure vessel. Air is supplied continuously. Cabin pressure is controlled mainly by regulating how much air leaves through the outflow valve.

⚡ Quick Recap

Pressure is controlled primarily at the outflow — not at the supply. The fuselage is the pressure vessel.

🏗️

4 — Structural Considerations

How pressurisation stresses the airframe

▼

Pressurisation creates stress because cabin pressure is higher than outside pressure. The fuselage must withstand this differential every flight.

Aircraft TypeTypical Differential
Small pressurised reciprocating aircraft~3.5 psi
High-performance jet aircraft~9 psi
⚠️ Warning

Repeated pressurisation and depressurisation cycles cause metal fatigue. Older aircraft require careful structural inspection.

⚙️

5 — Pressurisation Modes

Isobaric, constant differential, and control modes

▼

Isobaric Mode

Maintains a selected cabin altitude (e.g. 8,000 ft) as aircraft altitude changes. Used during normal operation until differential pressure limit is approached.

Constant Differential

Maintains a fixed pressure difference between cabin and outside air. Protects the aircraft structure from exceeding maximum differential pressure.

Control Modes

Automatic

Normal mode. Controller manages pressurisation automatically.

Standby

Backup automatic control if primary fails.

Manual

Crew directly controls outflow valve position. For abnormal ops or maintenance.

🧠 Memory Aid

Isobaric = constant cabin altitude (normal). Constant Differential = constant pressure gap (structural protection). Think: I hold altitude, C holds differential.

🖥️

6 — Cabin Pressure Controller

The brain of the pressurisation system

▼

The cabin pressure controller manages cabin pressure by controlling the outflow valve. It processes multiple inputs to determine the correct valve position.

Controller Inputs

Pressure Inputs

  • Cabin pressure
  • Ambient pressure
  • Barometric setting

Altitude / Flight Data

  • Selected flight altitude
  • Landing field altitude
  • ADC data
  • FMS flight plan data
💡 Key Concept

Modern systems are mostly automatic. Manual control remains available for abnormal operation or maintenance.

🔧

7 — Outflow Valve

Primary pressure control component

▼

The outflow valve is the main control valve for cabin pressure. It regulates how much air exits the cabin.

🔴
Valve Closes

Cabin pressure increases

🔵
Valve Modulates

Cabin pressure maintained

🟢
Valve Opens

Cabin pressure decreases

Valve Actuator Types

  • Pneumatic
  • Electric
  • Electro-pneumatic
  • Some systems: AC motors for normal, DC motors for standby/manual
⚡ Quick Recap

Outflow valve = primary pressure control. Closes → pressure up. Opens → pressure down. Modulates → pressure maintained.

🛡️

8 — Safety Valves & Protection Devices

Critical safety components — high exam importance

▼

Cabin Air Safety Valve

Prevents over-pressurisation.

Opens at: 8–10 psi differential.

Negative Pressure Relief Valve

Opens inward if outside pressure > cabin pressure.

Prevents structural damage and door opening difficulty.

Cabin Altitude Limiter

Closes outflow valves if cabin pressure becomes too low.

Prevents excessive cabin altitude increase.

Dump Valve

Rapidly releases cabin pressure.

Used for: abnormal events, maintenance, emergencies.

⚠️ Danger — Safety Critical

Excess Cabin Altitude Warning activates at ~10,000 ft cabin altitude. Crew must respond — may require emergency oxygen to prevent hypoxia.

⚠️ Note

The negative pressure relief valve protects against the outside pressure being greater than inside — which could prevent doors from opening and cause structural damage.

⚡ Quick Recap
  • Safety Valve: opens at 8–10 psi → prevents over-pressure
  • Neg. Pressure Relief: opens inward → protects structure
  • Altitude Limiter: closes outflow → prevents low pressure
  • Dump Valve: rapid release → emergencies / maintenance
  • Cabin Altitude Warning: ~10,000 ft → oxygen required

10 — Pressurisation Through the Flight

System behaviour at each phase of flight

▼
🛬

On Ground

WOW switch active. Safety valves held open. Aircraft not pressurised.

🛫

Takeoff

Throttle position + WOW signals initiate pressurisation. Outflow valves gradually close. Cabin pressure builds smoothly.

🌐

In Flight

Controller automatically follows the pressurisation schedule. Cabin altitude and differential pressure are regulated.

🛬

Landing

WOW switch activates again. Safety valves (and sometimes outflow valves) open. Ground pressurisation prevented in automatic mode.

💡 Key Concept — WOW Switch

The Weight-On-Wheels (WOW) switch signals whether the aircraft is on the ground or airborne. It is critical to the pressurisation logic — preventing pressurisation on the ground in automatic mode.

11 — Troubleshooting Principles

Approved maintenance approach for pressurisation faults

▼
⚠️ Warning

Never assume all pressurisation systems are identical. Always use the aircraft manufacturer's approved maintenance manual and fault isolation procedure.

Approved Procedure

  • Use the aircraft manufacturer's maintenance manual
  • Follow the approved fault isolation procedure
  • Check possible causes step by step
  • Use pressurisation test kits when required
  • A test flight may be required after maintenance

Common Faults

Control / Logic

  • Failure to pressurise
  • Failure to maintain pressurisation
  • Incorrect cabin rate
  • Controller or sensor faults

Mechanical

  • Valve malfunction
  • Leakage from seals
  • Pressure vessel area leaks

12 — Air Supply Sources

Bleed air, recirculation, turbo compressor, and air cycle systems

▼

Bleed Air

Taken from the engine compressor section. Used for pressurisation, air conditioning, engine starting, and anti-icing.

⚠ Reduces engine performance slightly.

Recirculation Fans

Large aircraft may reuse up to 50% of cabin air. Reduces bleed air demand.

Jet Pump (Venturi)

Bleed air through a venturi draws in outside air. Simple — no moving parts. Limited pressurisation volume; no direct cooling of ambient air.

Turbo Compressor

Bleed air drives a turbine → turbine drives a compressor → compresses outside air. Common on some turboprop aircraft.

💡 Air Cycle AC System

Common on transport aircraft. Uses bleed air, heat exchangers, a compressor, and an expansion turbine. Controls both cabin temperature and pressurisation.

⚡ Quick Recap
  • Bleed Air: from engine compressor — most common source
  • Recirculation: up to 50% of cabin air reused
  • Jet Pump: simple, no moving parts, limited capacity
  • Turbo Compressor: common on some turboprops
  • Air Cycle System: transport aircraft, controls temp + pressure
🔌

13 — APU & Ground Air Sources

Ground-based bleed air alternatives

▼

APU (Auxiliary Power Unit)

Provides bleed air for engine starting, air conditioning packs, and cabin cooling on the ground.

Ground Cart

Portable air source connected to the aircraft pneumatic system. Reduces APU and engine use. Useful for troubleshooting.

Ground Conditioned Air

Connected directly into the air distribution system. Aircraft packs do not need to operate — more economical during ground servicing or boarding.

🌀

14 — Air Distribution System

Flow path and distribution components

▼

Typical Airflow Path

  1. Conditioned air enters through ceiling vents
  2. Air circulates through the cabin
  3. Air exits through floor-level vents
  4. Air flows aft through lower areas
  5. Air leaves through outflow valves near the aft pressure bulkhead
💡 Key Concept

Large aircraft use temperature zones — different cabin areas have separate temperature control.

Key Distribution Components

Control Components

  • Trim air valves
  • Mixing chambers
  • Temperature sensors
  • Overheat switches

Flow Components

  • Inline fans
  • Jet pumps
  • Check valves
  • Gasper air nozzles
