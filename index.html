Pneumatic & Vacuum Systems

Concise revision material for high-pressure pneumatics, engine bleed air, vacuum systems, and pump types. Focus on components, safety, and exam-critical definitions.

Fluids

Both liquids and gases are fluids because both can flow.

Hydraulic Systems

Use liquid (almost incompressible). Provide smooth, precise, high-force operation.

Pneumatic Systems

Use compressed gas (air or nitrogen). Gas is highly compressible. Systems are lighter and simpler than hydraulics.

Common Elements: Both systems transmit power using lines, valves, and actuators.
Exam Tip: Pneumatics are commonly used for backup, emergency, and medium-power applications where hydraulics are not required.
Pressure Range
1,000 – 3,000 psi
Working Fluid
Compressed air or nitrogen

Main Uses

  • Emergency brakes
  • Door operation
  • Hydraulic pump drives
  • Alternators and starters
  • Water injection pumps
  • Emergency landing gear extension

One-Time Emergency

Use high-pressure bottles only. No in-flight recharge. Used once during emergencies, then depleted.

Rechargeable Systems

Use onboard compressors. Bottles recharge during flight. Allow repeated operation similar to hydraulics.

Air Compressor

Compresses air and recharges storage bottles.

Relief Valve

Prevents overpressure damage. Protects lines and seals from bursting.

Control / Selector Valve

Routes compressed air to selected components.

Check Valve

Allows one-way airflow only. Prevents backflow.

Restrictor

Fixed orifice that reduces airflow. Slows actuator movement.

Variable Restrictor

Adjustable airflow control. Allows precise speed regulation. Also called a needle valve.

Micron Filter

Uses replaceable filter cartridge. Includes bypass relief valve.

Screen Filter

Uses permanent wire screen. Cleaned by rotating the screen against scrapers.

Moisture Separator

Located downstream of the compressor.

Functions
  • Removes moisture from compressed air
  • Prevents corrosion and freezing
Main Parts
  • Reservoir
  • Dump valve
  • Pressure switch
  • Check valve
Chemical Dryer: Absorbs moisture inside lines and components. Cartridge color: Blue = serviceable. Any other color = replace.

Pneumatic Sources

  • Engine-driven compressors
  • Other onboard compressors
  • Ground air supply
  • Ground nitrogen supply

Storage Bottles

Usually lightweight wire-wrapped steel bottles. Pressure range: 1,000–3,000 psi.

Bottle Markings

Manufacture date, safe working pressure, last hydrostatic test date.

Standpipe

Prevents collected water from entering the system.

Maintenance

Bottles are regularly inspected and hydrostatically tested.

Charging Methods

  • Ground compressor
  • Ground air bottle
  • Nitrogen bottle
  • Onboard compressor

Charging Valve

Used to fill pneumatic bottles.

Control Valve

Traps air inside the bottle until needed.

Distribution: Uses steel or stainless-steel high-pressure lines. Check valves prevent reverse flow. Shuttle valves switch components from normal supply to emergency supply.

Pressure Regulator

Maintains correct system pressure.

Relief Valve

Protects system if regulator fails.

Check Valve

Prevents backflow toward compressors.

Isolation Valve

Isolates leaking or inactive sections.

Pressure-Reducing Valve

Reduces pressure for components requiring lower pressure.

Restrictors

Control actuator operating speed.

Safety-Critical: Pneumatic backup is often used for landing gear extension and brake operation.

Pneudraulics

High-pressure nitrogen pressurizes hydraulic fluid. Hydraulic fluid then moves the actuator.

Advantages
  • Combines pneumatic backup with hydraulic precision
  • Retains benefits of incompressible hydraulic fluid
Limitation: Hydraulic actuators using air are normally designed for hydraulic fluid only. Air use is limited to emergency one-time operation.
  • Pneumatic power drives a hydraulic pump
  • Hydraulic pump then supplies normal hydraulic pressure and flow
  • Maintains full hydraulic performance and control
  • Controlled through pneumatic valves selected from the cockpit

Regular Maintenance Tasks

  • Check compressor oil level regularly
  • Use correct specified oil
  • Inspect filler plugs and safety wire

System Purging

Performed periodically to remove oil, moisture, and contamination.

After Purging
  • Drain bottles
  • Recharge with clean, dry air or nitrogen
  • Perform leak checks and operational checks
Pressure Range
35 – 150 psi
Storage
No storage bottles required

Air supplied directly from engine or APU compressors.

The pneumatic manifold collects bleed air from engines, APU, or ground cart and distributes air throughout the aircraft.

Isolation Valves

Installed at engine/APU connections to isolate sections when required.

Engine starting

Wing anti-ice

Engine anti-ice

Cabin air conditioning

Air-driven hydraulic pumps

Hydraulic reservoir pressurization

Water tank pressurization

Probe heating systems

Ground Air Cart: Can supply pneumatic power through manifold connector and check valve.

Intermediate Stage

Normal air supply source.

High Stage

Used during high demand or low engine power conditions.

Regulating & Shutoff Valves: Maintain manifold pressure and airflow volume.

Modern Systems

Computer-controlled using pressure inputs, temperature inputs, engine status, and flight phase.

  • Automatic valve positioning
  • Automatic pressure regulation

Older Systems

Use purely pneumatic control:

  • Springs
  • Diaphragms
  • Pneumatic regulators

No electrical control required.

Protect ducts and manifolds from overpressure. Installed in manifold sections and APU ducts.

Large Ducts

Engine starters, wing anti-ice, air conditioning packs.

Small Ducts

Windscreen anti-fogging, probe heaters, small pneumatic users.

Temperature Control

  • Pneumatic manifold temperature may reach approximately 200°C
  • Heat exchangers cool bleed air before cabin use
  • Air conditioning packs further regulate cabin temperature

Ozone Converter

Reduces ozone entering the cabin using coated metal surfaces.

May Also Remove
  • Hydrocarbons
  • Odors
  • Carbon monoxide contaminants

Air Cleaners / Particle Separators

  • Remove airborne particles using centrifugal force
  • Use swirling airflow to separate contaminants
  • Purge valve removes collected dirt
  • Do not remove ozone

Vacuum Generation

Bleed air passes through a venturi to create suction.

Main Uses

Vacuum powers gyro instruments:

  • Artificial horizon
  • Directional gyro
  • Turn and bank indicator

Needle Valves

Reduce vacuum for instruments requiring lower suction.

System Layout

Flexible hoses connect instruments to vacuum manifold. Vacuum gauge monitors system suction.

General Principle

Purpose of pumps: increase pressure to perform work. Examples: move controls, pressurize cabins, start engines.

Basic Principle: Fluid forced out of discharge side. Partial vacuum created at inlet side. Atmospheric pressure pushes fluid into inlet.
Pressure & Vacuum Operation: Many pressure pumps also act as vacuum pumps. Inlet side = vacuum. Outlet side = pressure.

Positive Displacement

Deliver fixed volume per revolution. Constant output.

Examples

Vane pump, Piston pump

Non-Positive Displacement

Output varies with pressure due to internal slippage.

Example

Centrifugal impeller pump

Vane Pump (Wet / Dry)

Characteristics
  • Constant-displacement pump
  • Off-center rotor with sliding vanes
Operation
  • Expanding spaces draw air in
  • Contracting spaces force air out
Uses: Vacuum source for gyros, cabin pressurization.

Centrifugal Impeller Pump

Rotating impeller increases air velocity and pressure.

Uses
  • Turbochargers
  • Cabin pressurization
  • Engine supercharging

Multi-Stage Piston Pump

Produces very high pressures (>3,000 psi). Each stage compresses air further.

Example: Fokker F27 four-stage pneumatic pump.

Turbine Engine Compressor

Axial-flow (large aircraft). Centrifugal (smaller aircraft).

Supplies bleed air for cabin pressurization, anti-ice, hydraulic reservoir pressurization.

APU Compressor

Small centrifugal compressor in the APU. Provides air for air conditioning and air turbine engine starters.

Roots Blower

Two lobed rotors rotating closely together. Moves and compresses air without rotor contact. Used on some small aircraft for pressurization.

Vacuum Pumps

Usually vane-type pumps. Create suction for gyro instruments. Outlet air may be vented overboard or used for small pneumatic tasks.

Main Interface: Pneumatic systems commonly interact with hydraulic systems.

Emergency Brake Backup

  • High-pressure air or nitrogen may back up hydraulic brakes
  • Shuttle valve switches supply from hydraulic to pneumatic source
  • Hydraulic seals can tolerate emergency one-time pneumatic operation

Pneumatically Driven Hydraulic Pumps

  • Pneumatic air drives a hydraulic pump
  • Pump then powers the hydraulic system normally
  • Maintains hydraulic system characteristics: incompressible fluid, precise control
  • Activated through cockpit switch and pneumatic control valve

Main Monitored Parameter

Pneumatic manifold pressure.

Pressure Monitoring

Each manifold section uses a pressure transmitter. Dual indicators display left/right system pressures.

Isolation Valves

Used to isolate low-pressure or leaking sections.

Overtemperature Protection: Temperature switches installed in ducts may trip at approximately 255°C. Automatically close bleed valves and illuminate warning lights.

Modern Digital Systems

Computer-controlled monitoring includes pressure, temperature, valve positions, airflow, and leak detection.

  • Faulty sections automatically shut down
  • BITE (Built-In Test Equipment) reports faults to maintenance systems

Synoptic Display Information

  • Ground air in use
  • Duct pressures
  • Engine bleed air valve positions
  • Isolation valve positions
  • APU shutoff valve position
