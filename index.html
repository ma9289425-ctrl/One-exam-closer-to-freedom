Combustion Section

Gas turbine combustion chambers — types, functions, airflow, and key exam concepts for aviation students.

1
Introduction to the Combustion Section
What happens in the combustion chamber
💡 Key Concept
The combustion section is where fuel mixes with compressed air, combustion occurs, and heat energy is produced.

This energy is then used to drive the turbine, power the compressor, and produce thrust or shaft power. The combustion process greatly increases air temperature by releasing energy from the fuel-air mixture.

Purpose: The combustion section converts chemical energy (fuel) into thermal energy (heat), which powers all downstream engine functions.
⚡ Quick Recap
Fuel + compressed air → combustion → heat energy → drives turbine → powers compressor → produces thrust/shaft power.
2
Energy Distribution
How turbine energy is divided across engine types
💡 Key Concept
Approximately ⅔ (two-thirds) of turbine energy drives the compressor (gas generator). The remaining energy is used differently depending on engine type.
Engine TypeRemaining Energy Used For
TurbojetThrust from high-speed exhaust gases
TurbofanDriving the fan
TurbopropDriving a propeller
TurboshaftDriving helicopter rotor systems
⚠️ Exam Note
In a pure turbojet, most thrust comes directly from high-speed exhaust gases leaving the engine. In turbofan, turboprop, and turboshaft engines, much of the power drives fans, propellers, or rotors.
⚡ Quick Recap
⅔ energy → compressor. Remaining ⅓ → thrust (turbojet), fan (turbofan), propeller (turboprop), or rotors (turboshaft).
3
Main Functions of the Combustion Chamber
Four critical tasks the chamber must perform
The combustion chamber must perform four essential functions:

1️⃣ Mix Fuel & Air

Ensures efficient combustion and a stable flame.

2️⃣ Burn Efficiently

Converts chemical energy into heat energy.

3️⃣ Cool the Gases

Turbine blades cannot withstand the highest combustion temperatures directly.

4️⃣ Deliver to Turbine

Gases must flow smoothly, evenly, and at the correct temperature.

⚡ Quick Recap
4 Functions: Mix → Burn → Cool → Deliver. Remember: the chamber must reduce gas temperature before the turbine.
🧠 Memory Aid
MBCD — Mix, Burn, Cool, Deliver — the four jobs of the combustion chamber.
4
Location & Layout
Where the combustion section sits in the engine
💡 Key Concept
The combustion section is located between the compressor and the turbine.

Combustion chambers are arranged coaxially with the compressor and turbine. This arrangement allows smooth airflow through the engine.

⚡ Quick Recap
Compressor → Combustion Section → Turbine. Arranged coaxially for smooth airflow.
5
Main Components
Five basic elements found in all combustion chambers
All combustion chambers share the same five basic components:
  • Outer Casing — encloses the combustion chamber and supports components
  • Inner Liner — perforated, heat-resistant liner that contains the combustion flame
  • Fuel Injection System — sprays fuel into the chamber
  • Ignition System — spark igniters that start combustion during engine start
  • Fuel Drainage System — removes unburned fuel after shutdown
⚡ Quick Recap
5 components: Outer Casing, Inner Liner, Fuel Injection, Ignition System, Fuel Drainage.

7
Can Type Combustion Chamber
Separate cans, flame tubes, and ignition sequence
Common use: Turboshaft engines and Auxiliary Power Units (APUs).

Each combustion can contains an outer casing and an inner perforated liner.

Advantages: Easier maintenance; individual chamber replacement is possible.

💡 Flame Propagation Tubes
Older can-type engines use interconnector tubes (flame tubes). Since each can operates separately, these tubes spread the flame from one can to another during engine start.

🔥 Ignition Sequence

  1. Spark igniters ignite fuel in selected chambers
  2. Flame travels through interconnector tubes
  3. Remaining chambers ignite sequentially

Usually two igniter plugs are installed, located in the lower combustion chambers.

⚡ Quick Recap
Can type = separate chambers. Flame tubes spread ignition between cans. Two igniters, lower chambers. Used in turboshafts & APUs.
8
Fuel Drainage System
Why draining unburned fuel is safety-critical
The drainage system removes unburned fuel after shutdown.
🚨 Without Drainage — Problems
  • Gum Deposits: Fuel residue can block nozzles and contaminate manifolds
  • Afterfire Risk: Accumulated fuel may ignite unexpectedly during the next start
  • Excessive EGT: Too much fuel causes dangerous temperature rise and potential engine damage
📝 Exam Tip
Fuel drainage systems prevent afterfire and fuel residue buildup.
⚡ Quick Recap
Drainage removes unburned fuel → prevents gum deposits, afterfire, and excessive EGT.
9
Combustion Chamber Airflow
Primary and secondary air — roles and temperatures
Incoming compressed air is divided into two main streams:

Primary Air

  • Mixes with fuel
  • Supports combustion
  • Directed into the front section of the liner

Secondary Air

  • Cools combustion gases
  • Protects turbine components
  • Reduces temperature for turbine entry
⚠️ Critical Temperatures
Combustion temperatures can reach approximately 1,900°C. Secondary air cools gases to approximately 800°C before entering the turbine.
⚡ Quick Recap
Primary air = combustion. Secondary air = cooling (1,900°C → ~800°C). Both are essential for safe turbine operation.
10
Combustion Chamber Liner
Perforated design for airflow control and flame stability
The liner contains combustion, protects the outer casing, and controls airflow.

Perforations in the Liner

The liner contains holes, slots, and louvers. Each opening controls:

  • Air distribution
  • Flame stability
  • Cooling
⚡ Quick Recap
Liner = perforated heat-resistant shell. Holes/slots/louvers control air distribution, flame stability, and cooling.
11
Air/Fuel Ratio Limits
Rich limit, lean limit, and why the ratio matters
For proper combustion, fuel and air must remain within correct limits.

Rich Limit

Too much fuel, not enough oxygen → flame extinguishes.

Lean Limit

Too much air, mixture too weak → flame extinguishes.

🚨 Safety Critical
Both extremes (too rich or too lean) result in flame extinction. A stable combustion process requires the correct air/fuel ratio.
⚡ Quick Recap
Too rich = too much fuel → flame out. Too lean = too much air → flame out. Correct ratio = stable combustion.
