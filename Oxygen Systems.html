1 — Oxygen & the Atmosphere
Air composition, altitude effects, important gases
▼

Air Composition

🔵 Nitrogen

78%

Mostly inert — not directly used for breathing.

🟢 Oxygen

21%

Essential for human metabolism and combustion.

⚪ Other Gases

1%

CO₂, water vapour, ozone, and trace gases.

Effect of Altitude

💡 Key Concept
As altitude increases, total air pressure decreases. The percentage of oxygen in air stays approximately the same up to about 50 miles above Earth. The problem at altitude is lower oxygen pressure, not a lower oxygen percentage.

Important Atmospheric Gases

Carbon Dioxide (CO₂) ▼
Used by plants; helps regulate breathing in humans.
Water Vapour ▼
Varies with humidity. Important in weather formation.
Ozone (O₃) ▼
A form of oxygen with three atoms per molecule. Filters harmful ultraviolet radiation, protecting life on Earth.
⚡ Quick Recap
The atmosphere always contains approximately 21% oxygen. At altitude, lower pressure makes it harder for oxygen to enter the blood — this is the root cause of hypoxia.

2 — Human Respiration & Hypoxia
Why oxygen matters, effects of altitude, and aircraft solutions
▼
💡 Definition — Hypoxia
Hypoxia is a condition caused by insufficient oxygen reaching the body tissues. It is a primary risk at high altitude.

Effects of Hypoxia

  • Slower thinking and reaction time
  • Reduced physical performance
  • Poor judgment
  • Loss of consciousness at high altitude

Oxygen Saturation vs. Altitude

Altitude Approx. Saturation Likely Effects
Sea LevelNormalNormal function
Below 7,000 ftSufficientGenerally no impairment
10,000 ft~90%Headache, fatigue possible
15,000 ft~81%Sleepiness, blue lips/fingernails, poor judgment
22,000 ft~68%Significant impairment
25,000 ft~50%Unconsciousness possible after ~5 minutes

How Aircraft Prevent Hypoxia

Method 1

Increase Oxygen Pressure

Example: cabin pressurization — maintains a lower effective altitude inside the cabin.

Method 2

Increase Oxygen Quantity

Example: supplemental oxygen systems — deliver enriched oxygen directly to occupants.

⚡ Quick Recap
At altitude, oxygen absorption decreases because pressure decreases. Aircraft use pressurization, supplemental oxygen, or both to prevent hypoxia.

3 — Sources of Oxygen
Properties, oxidation, and production methods
▼

Oxygen Properties

At Normal Temp & Pressure
  • Colorless
  • Odorless
  • Tasteless
  • Exists as a gas
At −183°C

Oxygen becomes a liquid (pale blue in color).

⚠️ Critical Safety Point — Oxidation
Oxygen does NOT burn, but it strongly supports combustion. Pure oxygen can react violently with:
  • Fuel
  • Oil and grease
  • Petroleum products
This process (oxygen combining with other materials) is called oxidation.

Methods of Producing Oxygen

  • Liquefying air and removing nitrogen
  • Electrolysis of water
  • Molecular sieve systems
  • Chemical oxygen generators
⚡ Quick Recap
Oxygen is a colourless, odourless gas at normal conditions. It does not burn but vigorously supports combustion. Keep it away from oils, fuels, and grease at all times.

4 — Oxygen Storage Systems
Gaseous, chemical, LOX, and onboard generating systems
▼

Storage Methods Overview

Gaseous Oxygen

High-pressure cylinders. Most common in civil aviation.

Chemical / Solid

Sodium chlorate generators. Passenger emergency use.

LOX

Liquid oxygen at very low temperature. Mainly military.

Onboard Generating

Molecular sieve separates O₂ from air. Military / advanced systems.

Gaseous Oxygen Cylinders — Key Points

  • Usually painted green
  • Must contain aviator's breathing oxygen
  • Very low water content (prevents freezing)
  • Cylinders below 50 psi considered empty
  • Must be kept away from oil, grease, and fuel
Cylinder Materials
  • Steel
  • Aluminium
  • Kevlar-wrapped composite
⚠️ Hydrostatic Testing
Oxygen cylinders must be periodically tested to confirm they can safely hold pressure. The test checks for:
  • Leakage
  • Rupture
  • Permanent deformation
Always check hydrostatic test dates before use.

Chemical / Solid Oxygen Generators

💡 How It Works
Usually use sodium chlorate. When ignited, the material burns and produces oxygen as it does so.
  • Provide approximately 10–20 minutes of oxygen
  • Cannot be stopped once started
  • Used mainly for passenger emergency oxygen
  • Also used as backup systems in pressurised aircraft

Liquid Oxygen (LOX)

✅ Advantages
  • Stores large amount of oxygen in a small space
❌ Disadvantages
  • Difficult to handle
  • Expensive
  • Mainly military aviation
⚡ Quick Recap
Four storage methods exist. Cylinders must be green, contain aviator's breathing oxygen, and must be above 50 psi to be usable. Chemical generators cannot be stopped once activated and provide 10–20 minutes of O₂.

5 — Oxygen Charging Procedures
Safety rules, filler cart, step-by-step procedure, temperature compensation
▼
⚠️ Main Safety Rules — Before Charging
  • Follow the Aircraft Maintenance Manual (AMM)
  • Use only approved aviator's breathing oxygen
  • Open valves slowly
  • Fill slowly — avoid overheating
  • Purge air from hoses before filling
  • Check pressure frequently
  • Ground the oxygen cart to the aircraft

Oxygen Filler Cart

Contains large oxygen cylinders connected to a manifold. Oxygen flows naturally from high pressure → low pressure. No pump is normally required.

Step-by-Step Charging Procedure

  1. Check hydrostatic test dates on all cylinders.
  2. Check cylinder pressures on the filler cart.
  3. Ensure aircraft cylinder pressure is above 50 psi.
  4. Apply all oxygen safety precautions.
  5. Ground the cart to the aircraft.
  6. Connect hose and purge before filling.
  7. Open valves slowly.
  8. Fill using cylinders with progressively higher pressure.
  9. Close all valves after filling is complete.
  10. Disconnect, cap fittings, and remove grounding.

Temperature Compensation

🌡️ Temperature Effect on Pressure
Oxygen pressure changes with temperature. Always follow the temperature/pressure compensation chart:
  • Hot day: cylinder may be filled above normal rated pressure (per chart).
  • Cold day: cylinder is filled below normal rated pressure to allow for pressure rise later.
⚡ Quick Recap
Always follow the AMM. Ground the cart, purge the hose, open valves slowly. Use progressively higher pressure cylinders. Compensate for temperature using the reference chart.

6 — Oxygen Distribution
Tubing, valves, and adiabatic compression hazard
▼

Tubing & Fittings

Line TypeMaterial
High-pressure linesStainless steel
Low-pressure linesAluminium
Mask hosesFlexible plastic

Oxygen tubing is identified with a green "BREATHING OXYGEN" marking.

Common Valves in High-Pressure Systems

Filler Valve

Entry point for charging the system.

Check Valve

Prevents backflow of oxygen.

Shutoff Valve

Isolates the oxygen supply.

Pressure Reducer

Steps down pressure for distribution.

Pressure Relief Valve

Protects against over-pressurisation.

🔥 Critical Hazard — Adiabatic Compression
Adiabatic compression occurs when high-pressure oxygen rushes rapidly into a low-pressure area and compresses, generating intense heat.

This heat can cause ignition — even in materials that do not normally burn easily.

Safety Rule: Always open oxygen valves SLOWLY.
⚡ Quick Recap
High-pressure lines: stainless steel. Low-pressure: aluminium. All oxygen tubing is green-marked. Adiabatic compression is a serious ignition hazard — always open valves slowly.

Key Terms & Definitions
Exam-essential vocabulary for ATA 35
▼
Hypoxia
Insufficient oxygen reaching body tissues; caused by low oxygen pressure at altitude.
Oxidation
The process of oxygen combining with another substance. Can be slow (rust) or rapid (combustion).
Aviator's Breathing Oxygen
Oxygen specifically approved for aircraft use; must have very low water content to prevent system freezing.
LOX (Liquid Oxygen)
Oxygen stored in liquid form at very low temperature (−183°C); pale blue in colour. Mainly military use.
Molecular Sieve
A device that separates oxygen from ambient air for use in onboard oxygen generating systems (OBOGS).
Adiabatic Compression
Heat generated when high-pressure oxygen rapidly enters a low-pressure space. Can cause ignition — always open valves slowly.
Ozone (O₃)
A form of oxygen with three atoms per molecule. Found in the upper atmosphere; filters harmful UV radiation.
Sodium Chlorate
Chemical used in solid oxygen generators. Produces oxygen when ignited; process cannot be stopped once started.
Hydrostatic Testing
Periodic pressure test of oxygen cylinders to detect leakage, rupture, or permanent deformation.
OBOGS
Onboard Oxygen Generating System — uses a molecular sieve to continuously produce oxygen from air.
