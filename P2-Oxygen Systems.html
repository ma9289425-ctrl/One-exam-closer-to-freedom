Overview
Oxygen System Types at a Glance
Compare the five major system types used in aviation
▾
🔵
Continuous-Flow
Oxygen flows constantly. Simple and reliable. Less efficient — wastes O₂ during exhalation.
🟢
Demand-Flow
Oxygen delivered only during inhalation. More efficient. Two subtypes: diluter-demand and pressure-demand.
⚗️
Chemical (Solid-State)
Oxygen generated by chemical reaction. Compact, lightweight. Used for passenger emergency oxygen.
⚡
Electronic Pulse Demand
Electronically detects inhalation and delivers timed pulses. Extends oxygen duration.
❄️
LOX (Liquid Oxygen)
Stores oxygen in liquid form, converts to gas before delivery. Mainly military use.
💡 Key Design Factors
Aircraft type · Operating altitude · Whether the aircraft is pressurized — these determine which system is used.
🔵
Section 4.1
Continuous-Flow Systems
Oxygen flows constantly once the valve is opened — simple but less efficient
▾
✅ Advantages
  • Simple design
  • Reliable operation
  • Common in smaller aircraft
❌ Disadvantages
  • Wastes oxygen during exhalation
  • Shorter oxygen duration
🔧 Regulator Types
Type How It Works
Manual Crew adjusts oxygen flow manually as altitude changes
Automatic Uses an aneroid device that automatically increases flow as altitude increases
😷 Rebreather Systems

Rebreather masks capture exhaled oxygen that is still usable, store it in a bag or reservoir, and allow it to be inhaled again — reducing O₂ waste.

🧑‍✈️ Passenger Emergency Oxygen (Pressurized Airliners)
  • Masks stored in overhead Passenger Service Units (PSUs)
  • Cabin depressurization automatically deploys masks
  • Pulling the mask activates oxygen flow
  • Masks fit snugly but are not airtight — allow cabin air mixing via vent holes

⚡ Quick Recap — Continuous-Flow
Oxygen flows continuously once the valve is opened — even during exhalation. Simple and reliable but less efficient than demand-flow systems.
🟢
Section 4.2
Demand-Flow Systems
Oxygen flows only during inhalation — more efficient than continuous-flow
▾
💡 Core Principle
When the user inhales → oxygen flows. When the user exhales → oxygen flow stops. Result: oxygen is conserved and duration is extended.

4.2.1 Diluter-Demand System

⚙️ How It Works
  • Delivers oxygen only during inhalation
  • Mixes oxygen with cabin air automatically
  • As altitude increases → more oxygen, less cabin air mixed in
  • At approximately 34,000 ft → supplies nearly 100% oxygen
🚨 Emergency Mode
  • Pilot can manually select 100% oxygen
  • Or select emergency continuous-flow oxygen
⚡ Quick Recap — Diluter-Demand
Conserves oxygen + automatically adjusts O₂ concentration with altitude. At ~34,000 ft → ~100% oxygen.

4.2.2 Pressure-Demand System

⚙️ How It Works

Operates like a diluter-demand system, but oxygen is delivered under positive pressure — forcing oxygen into the lungs.

  • At very high altitude, normal breathing pressure becomes insufficient
  • Pressurized oxygen ensures proper blood saturation
  • Used above approximately 40,000 ft
  • Common in airliners and high-performance aircraft
⚡ Quick Recap — Pressure-Demand
Delivers oxygen under pressure for very high-altitude operations where normal breathing mechanics are insufficient.

4.2.3 Cockpit Oxygen System Layout

🔩 Typical Components
  • Remotely mounted oxygen cylinders
  • Pressure reducer
  • Shutoff valve
  • Distribution tubing
  • Individual crew regulators
  • Crew oxygen masks
✈ Crew Control Capability
  • Turn system on/off
  • Select oxygen mode
  • Monitor oxygen pressure
  • Transport aircraft: crew and passenger systems are typically separate
📊 Demand-Flow Comparison
Feature Diluter-Demand Pressure-Demand
Delivery On inhalation only On inhalation under positive pressure
Air mixing Mixes O₂ with cabin air Same, but delivered under pressure
Altitude Up to ~34,000 ft Above ~40,000 ft
Application General aviation / commercial crew Airliners / high-performance aircraft
⚗️
Section 4.3
Chemical Oxygen Systems
Solid-state generators used mainly for passenger emergency oxygen
▾
⚙️ Operation

Pulling the mask down fires a firing mechanism that ignites the chemical generator. Oxygen production begins immediately.

  • Once activated, cannot be stopped
  • Entire generator must be replaced after use
  • Provides oxygen for approximately 10–20 minutes
  • Sufficient time for emergency descent
✅ Advantages
  • Lightweight
  • Compact
  • Minimal maintenance
  • Long shelf life
❌ Limitations
  • Cannot be stopped once activated
  • Full generator replacement required after each use
⚠️ Warning
Chemical oxygen generators produce heat during operation. Handle with caution. Once activated, the reaction cannot be interrupted.
⚡ Quick Recap — Chemical Oxygen
Used primarily for passenger emergency oxygen. Provides 10–20 minutes of oxygen. Cannot be stopped once activated.
⚡
Section 4.4
Electronic Pulse Demand Systems (EDS)
Electronically optimised oxygen delivery to extend supply duration
▾
⚙️ Operation
  • Detects inhalation electronically
  • Releases oxygen in timed pulses
  • Automatically adjusts delivery with altitude
  • Available as portable or permanently installed units
🔒 Backup Feature

Most EDS units include an emergency bypass for continuous flow if electronics fail — ensuring uninterrupted oxygen supply.

⚡ Quick Recap — EDS
Electronically timed pulses synchronised with inhalation → reduces waste and extends oxygen duration. Emergency bypass available if electronics fail.
❄️
Section 4.5
LOX — Liquid Oxygen Systems
Liquid oxygen stored and converted to gas before delivery — mainly military use
▾
🔩 Characteristics
  • Stores oxygen in liquid form
  • Converts to gaseous oxygen before delivery
  • Requires special insulated containers
  • Includes heat exchange assemblies for gas conversion
  • Fitted with pressure relief protection
💡 Usage
LOX systems are mostly found in military aircraft or former military aircraft in civilian use.
🟡
Section 5
Oxygen Indication and Warnings
Blowout disks, flow indicators, pressure gauges, and pulse oximeters
▾
🟢 Blowout Disks

High-pressure systems include relief valves and blowout disks to release excess pressure safely.

  • Most blowout disks are green
  • If the disk is missing → the relief valve may have opened
⚠️ Missing Green Disk
A missing green blowout disk indicates the system may have experienced an overpressure event. The cause must be investigated before flight.

5.1 Flow Indicators

⚙️ Purpose & Types

Flow indicators confirm that oxygen is actually flowing to the user.

  • Moving pith balls
  • Blinking indicators
  • Adjustable flow meters

They provide quick confirmation that the system is operating correctly.

5.2 Pressure Indications

📊 Oxygen Quantity Monitoring

Cylinder pressure indicates remaining oxygen quantity. Sources include:

  • Direct gauges — on the cylinder or panel
  • Electrical transducers — remote sensing
  • Multifunction cockpit displays — integrated indication

5.3 Pulse Oximeters

🩸 Purpose

Portable pulse oximeters measure blood oxygen saturation and heart rate. Pilots may use them to monitor oxygen needs and detect early signs of hypoxia.

⚡ Quick Recap — Section 5
  • Green blowout disk missing = overpressure event → investigate before flight
  • Flow indicators confirm oxygen is reaching the user
  • Cylinder pressure = remaining quantity (gauge, transducer, or MFD)
  • Pulse oximeters monitor blood O₂ saturation and heart rate

Section 6
Oxygen System Maintenance
Safety-critical procedures — leak testing, draining, purging, and component care
▾

6.1 Leak Testing

🔍 Detection Method
  • Use oxygen-safe leak detection fluid only
  • Bubble formation indicates leakage at a fitting or connection
📋 Critical Rules
  • Never overtighten fittings — use proper torque
  • Replace damaged fittings
  • Depressurize the system before performing any repairs
🚨 High-Pressure Hazard
High-pressure sections may contain up to 1,850 psi. Always isolate and depressurize before maintenance. Never work on a pressurized oxygen system.

6.2 Draining an Oxygen System

⚙️ Procedure
  1. 1Close the cylinder valve
  2. 2Allow oxygen to flow from masks or outlets
  3. 3Drain remaining pressure safely
⚠️ Draining Safety
Release oxygen outdoors and away from all flames and ignition sources. Never vent oxygen in an enclosed space.

6.3 Purging an Oxygen System

🎯 Purpose

Purging removes moisture, contaminants, and air introduced during maintenance.

🚨 Why Moisture Is Dangerous
Moisture inside oxygen systems can:
  • Freeze at altitude — blocking valves and regulators
  • Cause corrosion of system components
⚙️ Purging Process
  • Run oxygen through the system for several minutes
  • Restore oxygen saturation inside the system
  • Final purge must always use pure oxygen
⚡ Quick Recap — Purging
Purging removes moisture and contaminants. Moisture can freeze or corrode components. Final purge must use pure oxygen.

6.4 Inspection of Masks and Hoses

🔍 Inspection Items
  • Leaks, holes, and cracks
  • Damaged straps
  • Faulty microphones
  • Cleanliness — free from petroleum contamination
😷 Smoke Masks
  • Protect both eyes and respiratory system
  • Include built-in microphones
  • Connect to demand regulators

6.5 Replacement of Tubing, Valves, and Fittings

🧹 Cleanliness Requirement

All oxygen components must be clean, dry, and free from oil or grease — contamination is a serious fire hazard.

🧪 Approved Cleaning Agents

Non-petroleum cleaners such as acetone and trichloroethylene may be used — follow manufacturer procedures.

🔩 Sealants
  • Only oxygen-compatible sealants may be used
  • Teflon tape may be used carefully — start at least two threads from the end

6.6 Oxygen System Safety

🚨 Major Hazard — Fire Risk
Pure oxygen greatly increases fire risk. Any ignition source near oxygen can cause a catastrophic fire.
📋 Critical Safety Rules
  • No smoking within 50 ft of oxygen systems
  • Keep away from oil, grease, and fuel at all times
  • Use clean, oxygen-compatible tools and equipment
  • Work in ventilated areas
  • Use only approved oxygen-compatible materials
  • Keep protective caps installed when not connected
  • Open valves slowly
  • Perform pressure and leak checks after all maintenance
🏪 Cylinder Storage
  • Store in cool, ventilated areas
  • Away from heat sources
  • Away from petroleum products
⚡ Quick Recap — Maintenance Safety
Oxygen is a powerful oxidiser. No smoking within 50 ft. No oil or grease. Depressurize before work. Open valves slowly. Final leak check mandatory.

Key Terms & Definitions
Essential vocabulary for your exam
▾
Continuous-flow system
Oxygen flows continuously once the valve is opened — even during exhalation.
Demand-flow system
Oxygen flows only during inhalation — conserves oxygen supply.
Diluter-demand regulator
Mixes oxygen with cabin air; automatically adjusts concentration with altitude.
Pressure-demand regulator
Delivers oxygen under positive pressure — required above ~40,000 ft.
Aneroid device
Altitude-sensing device used in automatic continuous-flow regulators to increase flow as altitude rises.
Blowout disk
Green safety disk that ruptures to indicate an overpressure release event. Missing disk = investigate before flight.
Pulse oximeter
Portable device measuring blood oxygen saturation and heart rate.
Purging
Process of removing moisture and contaminants from the oxygen system using pure oxygen.
LOX
Liquid Oxygen — stored in liquid form, converted to gas before delivery. Mainly military use.
EDS
Electronic Pulse Demand System — detects inhalation electronically and delivers oxygen in timed pulses.
PSU
Passenger Service Unit — overhead unit housing passenger emergency oxygen masks.
Calibrated orifice
Fixed opening that controls the precise flow rate of oxygen to each mask outlet.
